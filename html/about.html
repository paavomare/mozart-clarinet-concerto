Introduction

The Clarinet Concerto belongs to the golden repertoire of this instrument and "represents a triumphant manifestation of the widespread eighteenth-century desire to appeal both to amateurs of music and to connoisseurs." [3, p. IX] The creation of the masterpiece was decisively influenced by the friendship of Mozart with Anton Stadler who was a great clarinet virtuoso of his time. Stadler owned a special sort of instrument considered to be the basset clarinet, but the original is unfortunately lost. The Concerto was aimed to be performed on this instrument. However, after a publication of an arrangement for clarinet in A in 1801 [2, preface] the Concerto has been predominantly playing in this version, sometimes, for educational purpose, even on the clarinet in B flat with transposed piano reduction. This version is considered as 'traditional', although it is certainly not the original one.

Project description

The edition by Franz Giegling [1] provides a reconstructed version for basset clarinet and a version for clarinet in A. The aim of the current encoding was an exact reproduction of the second version. Giegling's edition is based on 14 sources including (piecewise) autographs, prints, copies, arrangements and one review. In the Critical Report the editor provides an index of alternative readings ('Leseartenverzeichnis') with the reference to the sources. An implementing of this index could be an issue for further enhancement of the encoding as well as merging of the versions for basset clarinet and clarinet in A.

Two fermatas in bars 127 and 315 of the first movement can be used as opportunity for a cadenza. However, rather for a "modest cadential ornamentation [...] than [for] an extended improvisation" [3, p. 75]. Giegling provides some proposals in these bars. Additional cadenzas by Meyer/Wehle are included in the encoding for technical demonstration of choice possibilities only. However, this section could be extended with other suggestions from these clarinetists as well as with cadenzas from earlier authors such as Stanislav Krticka, Jacque Ibert, Karlheinz Stockhausen [3, pp. 75-76], etc.
